Osteoarthritis

Progressive disease

  • Degenerative, wear-and-tear
  • Inflammation of synovial membrane
    • increased production of synovial fluid (synovial effusion) & joint swelling
  • OA synovial fluid less viscous
    • Normal synovial fluid - clear, pale yellow, viscous (due to hyaluronic acid) with small number of WBCs

Characteristic symptoms of OA

  • Pain occurs with activity, improves with rest
  • Morning stiffness absent or lasts < 30 minutes (short periods of stiffness after inactivity)
  • Severe OA: pain at night, at rest

Diagnosis of Hand OA

Handpain, aching, stiffness + 3 of 4 features:

  • Hard tissue enlargement of 2 or more in 10 selected joints
    • 2nd & 3rd PIP & DIP joints (4*2)
    • 1st CMC - base of thumb (1*2)
      • Total of 10 (both hands counted)
  • Hard tissue enlargement of 2 or more DIP joints
  • Fewer than 3 swollen MCP joints
  • Deformity of at least 1 of 10 selected joints

Diagnosis of Knee OA

Knee pain + at least 5 of following:

  • Age > 50
  • Stiffness < 30 minutes
  • Crepitus
  • Osteophytes
  • Bony tenderness
  • Bony enlargement
  • No palpable warmth
  • ESR < 40 mm/h
  • RF < 1:40
  • Synovial fluid - clear, viscous or WCC < 2000/mm3

Diagnosis of Hip OA

Hip pain + at least 2 of 3 features:

  • ESR < 20mm/h
  • Radiographic femoral or acetabular osteophytes
  • Radiographic joint space narrowing (superior, axial and/or medial)

Disease of joint organ

Imaging OA

L - loss of joint space
O - Osteophyte formation
S - Subchondral sclerosis (thickening)
S - Subchondral cysts

Specific Changes

  • Symmetrical
  • DIPs (especially 2 & 3)
  • IP thumb
  • CMC thumb - “base of thumb”
  • PIPs possible
  • scaphotrapezial possible
  • Heberden/Bouchard nodes

Management

Modifiable risk factors

  • WEIGHT LOSS - EXERCISE

Surgery & OA

  • Arthroscopic Surgery
    • No direct benefit
  • Joint replacement (Hip, Knee)
    • Advanced OA (clinically & radiologically)
    • Failure of non-operative treatment
    • Preferably age > 55
      • Improved QoL
      • Reduced pain
      • Increased activity, fitness, longevity
      • Hips slightly more successful than knees

Osteomyelitis

Infection of bone

  • Caused by pyogenic bacteria or mycobacteria

Associations

  • Open fractures
  • Haematogenous spread
    • S aureus, beta-haemolytic Strep
    • Enterobacter in elderly, immunocompromised
    • Iatrogenic
      • Cannulae
      • IVDU
      • Dialysis
      • Endocarditis
      • Urinary catheter
    • Primary infection in children at metaphysis
    • Vertebrae commonly infected for adults
  • Diabetes
  • Peripheral vascular disease

Symptoms

  • Fever, chills, fatigue (INFECTION)
  • Localised pain, swelling, redness (Inflammation)

Examination

  • Decreased RoM, deformity
  • Focal tenderness
  • Unable to weight bear

Investigations

  • ESR, CRP elevated
  • WCC elevated
    • Chronic osteomyelitis - normal WCC
  • Blood cultures most vital to guide diagnosis & therapy
  • Bone biopsy whenever possible
  • Superficial cultures misleading
    • Unless S. aureus/beta-haemolytic Strep

Imaging

  • X-Ray

    • Periosteal elevation
    • Bone irregularity
    • Osteolysis
    • New bone formation

  • MRI

    • Ableto detect abnormalities in bone marrow
    • MRI > CT > X-ray

Septic arthritis

  • Bacterial
    • Usually haematogenous
      • endocarditis
      • meningococcus
      • disseminated gonorrhea
      • prosthetic limb (risk of seeding bacteria)
  • Viral
    • involves multiple joints (systemic infection)
  • TB, fungi
    • chronic, slowly progressive, monoarticular arthritis
    • systemic/local infection at remote sites

Symptoms

  • fever variable
  • focal pain the only symptom of axial infection
  • decreased RoM, swelling, redness, warmth
  • pain, loss of function over 1-2 weeks

Examination

  • focal joint tenderness, inflammation, effusion
  • RoM limited, both active & passive
  • discomfort, focal tenderness
  • Children with hip infection
    • Hip flexion, external rotation, resist RoM

Microbiology

  • S aureus
    • multifocal - endocarditis
    • costo-chondral, sternoclavicular - endocarditis
  • N gonorrhoeae
    • disseminated gono
      • mono or poly arthropathy
      • migratory polyarthralgia (multiple joint pain) without purulent joint infection with wrist pain & dermatitis
        Antidote for paracetamol - N acetyl cysteine

Investigation

  • WCC
  • Gram stain
  • Culture aspirate
  • Blood culture
  • PCR for N. gono
    • urinary specimens
  • Serology
    • viruses, syphilis, brucella

UV Photobiology

UVA & UVB are immunosuppressive

  • UV immunosuppression key role in developing skin cancers
    UV-induced DNA damage
  • triggers immunosuppression & carcinogenesis
    • DNA repair process to combat DNA damage
    • Energy intensive
    • Nicotinamide (amide form of Vitamin B3, niacin is acid form)
      • Increases cellular ATP for DNA repair

Wavelengths
UVB - 290 - 230 nm

  • 297 nm UVB more damaging than 313 nm UVB

UVA2 - 320 - 340 nm
UVA1 - 340 - 400 nm

UVB more damaging than UVA

  • UVB directly damages DNA
    • disrupts DNA helix by making consecutive bases join together instead of normal adjacent base-pairing against opposite strand
      • cyclobutane pyrimidine dimer (CPD)
  • UVA causes oxidative damage on DNA
    • formation of ROS in dermal skin layer
    • ROS does oxidative damage to DNA

UVA exposure more than UVB over time – Amount of UVA > UVB at ground level

  • UVA still can cause damage – lower dps but over a longer time exposure

Pellagra

Vitamin B3 deficiency (niacin)

  • Diarrhoea
  • Dementia
  • Death - 40% mortality

Melanoma

Melanocytes (pigment-containing cells)
Sometimes develop from moles

  • increase in size
  • irregular edges
  • change in colour
  • itchiness
  • skin breakdown

Basal cell carcinoma (BCC)

Most common skin cancer

  • Slow-growing, almost never metastasises
    • Superficial - nodular, pigmented
    • Micronodular - infiltrating, morphoeic (sclerosing i.e. thickening)

Actinic Keratoses

Sunspots
Pre-cancer

  • can progress to squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)
  • can spontaneously resolve
  • tenderness might indicate progression

Bowen’s Disease (SCC in situ)

‘Early stage SCC‘

  • gradually enlarging, well-demarcated red plaque
  • irregular border with surface crusting or scaling
  • potentially malignant

Keratoacanthoma

Common, low-grade skin tumour

  • originates from neck of hair follicles
    • Dome-shaped, symmetrical
    • Smooth wall of inflamed skin, covered with keratin scales & debris
  • unlikely to invade/metastasise

Squamous cell carcinoma (SCC)

Less common than BCC
CAN metastasise

  • lymph nodes
  • lungs
  • bone

Xeroderma pigmentosum (XP)

Autosomal recessive
Inability to repair UV induced damage

Gorlin syndrome

Autosomal dominant
Abnormal PTCH tumour suppressor gene
Predisposition to BCC

Dysplastic naevus syndrome

Unusual naevi (moles)

  • large amount of moles
  • multiple inherited melanomas

Pathology

Paget’s disease

Disordered bone resorption & formation
Mosaic pattern histologically - osteoclasts with more than 10 nuclei

Treatment
Bisphosphonates
