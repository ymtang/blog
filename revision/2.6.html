Crystalarthropathy

Gout

Deposition of monosodium urate crystals in the joint, causing painful arthritis

  • Due to over-production/under-secretion of uric acid

Uric acid - a purine base derived from breakdown of purines (2 rings)

  • adenine, guanine, hypoxanthine
    • Xanthine oxidase acts on hypoxanthine & xanthine to form uric acid
      • Allopurinol (inhibits xanthine oxidase)
  • Normal uric acid (urate) concentration
    • Male - 0.25 - 0.42 mmol/L
    • Female - 0.18 - 0.38 mmol/L
  • Uric acid solubility at 0.4 mmol/L
  • Crystallisation occurs > 0.4 mmol/L

Dietary sources of uric acid

  • Seafood, meat, alcohol (especially beer)

De novo purine synthesis pathway (i.e. making new purines)
PRPP - phosphoribosyl pyrophosphate

  • Promotes uric acid production

Salvage pathway (i.e. makes the purine base become nucleotides instead of going toward uric acid synthesis)
APRT - adenine phosphoribosyltransferase

  • Inhibits uric acid production
  • Reciver purine bases for use as purine nucleotides

Lesch-Nyhan Syndrome (juvenile gout)

X-linked recessive
Gene mutation leads to deficient HGPRT - hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase

  • HGPRT responsible for salvage pathway

Promotion of de novo pathway with PRPP

  • Serious CNS effects
    • mental retardation
    • growth retardation
    • choreoathetosis (involuntary movements - twisting, writhing)
    • spasticity
    • self-mutilation
      Neurons have low levels of de novo purine synthesis enzymes

Benefits of uric acid

Anti-oxidant effect

Interaction of urate crystals and immune system

  • low grade inflammation (raised WCC in synovial fluid) between flares
  • Escalates during flares
  • Crystals:
    • Recognised by TLRs
    • Phagocytosed by macrophages
    • Stimulates IL-1B -> TNF, IL-6, neutrophil chemottractants
      • Inflammation
    • Bone lesions (from arthritis) - activation of macrophages/osteoclasts

Clinical Features

Early gout

  • monoarticular
  • rapid onset, severe pain
  • 1st MTP (podagra - big toe pain) or mid-foot

Late gout

  • Tophi
  • Oligoarticular attacks (multiple joints affected)
    • Knees, writs, fingers, elbows
  • Prolonged episodes -> incomplete resolution

Diagnosis (distinguish)

Joint aspiration & synovial fluid analysis

  • Gout - identify monosodium urate monohydrate
    • Needle-shaped
    • negatively birefringent
      • Corticosteroid crystals such as from betamethasone look like MSU crystals
  • Pseudogout - identify calcium pyrophosphate
    • Rhomboidal
    • positively birefringent
  • Septic arthritis – excluded with synovial fluid analysis from aspiration
  • Other types of inflammatory arthritis (RA)

Serum urate not useful for dx

  • Up to 40% patients with acute gout have serum urate below crystallisation threshold of 0.42mmol/L
  • Only 20% patients above 0.42mmol/L threshold develop gout

X-ray

  • Insensitive
  • Soft tissue swelling
  • Cloud-like calcification
  • Preservation of joint space
  • Erosion present (commonly mistaken for RA)

Crystal arthritis & sepsis can co-exist!

Management of gout

Controlling uric-acid levels (not just with gout)

Not urgent, but long-term commitment for gout patients

  • Indications for urate-lowering therapy:
    • tophi
    • chronic joint, tendon damage from gout
      • erosions (x-ray)
      • chronic gouty arthropathy (clinical evidence)
      • persistent symptoms instead of intermittent
      • multiple joint involvement
    • chronic kidney disease (stage II)
    • Hx of kidney stones
    • Patient’s choice
      • based on frequency (>= 2 attacks a year), severity & duration of gout attacks
  • Target uric acid levels
    • < 0.36 mmol/L
      • This number is well below all laboratory reference ranges
    • < 0.30 mmol/L
      • Patients with tophi or with evidence of high urate crystal burden

Prevent acute gout (TARGET LEVELS of uric acid < 0.36 mmol/L)
Eliminate tophi – deposits of monosodium urate (cause inflammation -> arthritis)

  • Block uric acid synthesis
    • Allopurinol - binds irreversibly to xanthine oxidase (suicide inhibition)
    • Febuxostat - (non-purine inhibitor of xanthine oxidase)
  • Promote urinary excretion of uric acid
  • Convert to allantoin (highly water soluble)
    • In other animals, uric acid is converted to allantoin via uricase
    • Humans lack uricase
      • rasburicase - derived from fungi
  • Tumour lysis syndrome - when high levels of apoptosis occurs during chemotherapy, cells release their contents, plasma urate levels increased
  • Reverse hyperuricaemia in ischaemic heart disease

Pharmacotherapy

NSAIDs

  • acute attacks
    • diclofenac, indometacin or other NSAIDs
    • NOT WITH ASPIRIN
      • actually raises plasma urate levels at low doses
        • inhibits uric acid secretion in renal tubules
  • Contraindications
    • Renal impairment
    • Cardiac/hepatic failure
    • Bleeding risk due to anti-coagulative effects
    • Peptic ulceration - COX-1 inhibition
    • Poorly controlled hypertension

Colchicine (rarely used alone)
Modulates pro-inflammatory & anti-inflammatory processes

  • prevents microtubule assembly
    • disrupts inflammasome activation
    • microtubule-based inflammatory cell chemotaxis
      • leukocytes thus unable to migrate to sites of uric acid deposition, reducing inflammatory responses
  • disrupts generation of leukotrienes, cytokines
  • disrupts phagocytosis

Colchicine in Acute gout

  • Normal dosing (initial high dose of 1mg, half dose 0.5mg every 2-6 hours)
    • high rates of GI toxicity
  • Low dosing (initial higher dose of 1.2mg, half dose 0.6mg 1 hour later)
    • reduced GI toxicity but relatively poor efficacy
  • Prophylactic dose
    • 0.5mg twice daily

Colchicine toxicity

  • nausea, diarrhoea, vomiting
  • acute myopathy, multi-system failure
    • beware patients with renal failure
    • beware patients on drugs that inihibit cytochrome P450 3A4
      • cyclosporin, pravastatin, azithromycin, clarithromycin

Allopurinol (prophylactic)
Lowers plasma urate by irreversibly binding to xanthine oxidase (suicide inhibition)

  • xanthine unable to be converted to uric acid
  • 300mg/day will reach target level
  • 600mg, 900mg in patients with normal renal function
    • further decrease in serum urate
      Useful for patients with recurrent gout attacks

Severe allupurionol hypersensitivity

  • 1 in 1000
  • Occurs < 6 weeks after starting allopurinol
  • Not associated with higher maintenance doses
  • Risk factors
    • renal insufficiency (increased age)
      • Start low dose (1.5mg per unit of eGFR daily)
      • Warn patient, monitor for hypersensitivity in first 6-12 weeks
      • Progressively increase dose until maximal effect reached
    • Thiazide diuretic use
    • Initial high dose
    • HLA-B*58:01 (patients with GFR < 60)
      • Han Chinese/Thai/Korean extraction
  • Desensitisation to allopurinol
    • oral dosing, increasing from minute amounts to 300mg/day over 30 days

Probenecid (uricosuric)

  • increases renal urate clearance
  • Need good hydration, urine to be alkalinised
    • Alkaline pH, urate less soluble, tend to crystallise in urine
  • Requires renal sufficiency
  • Useful addition to allopurinol
  • Next option for patients intolerant to allopurinol

Febuxostat

  • Xanthine oxidase inhibitor - same function as allopurinol
  • Up to 80mg/day
  • Rapid oral absorption
  • Metabolised in liver
  • No dose modification in patients with moderate renal impairment
  • Good alternative for patients intolerant to allopurinol

Benzbromarone

  • increases renal urate clearance
  • Need good hydration, urine to be alkalinised
    • Alkaline pH, urate less soluble, tend to crystallise in urine
  • Requires renal sufficiency
  • REQUIRES CLOSE MONITORING OF LFTs
    • Liver failure deaths occurred
  • Potent uricosuric, can be added to allopurinol

Other uricosuric drugs (prophylactic)
Sulfinpyrazone

Increased secretion of uric acid

  • inhibit reabsorption at renal tubules

  • Fluid status IMPORTANT

    • to prevent crystallisation in urine

Less effective & more toxic than allopurinol

  • Normally used when patient unable to tolerate allopurinol

Uricase derivatives – converts uric acid to water-soluble allantoin
Rasburicase

  • treat/prevent uric acid nephropathy in chemotherapy
    • tumour lysis syndrome
  • acts on BOTH soluble urate and urate crystals
    • rapid reduction of tophi
  • Expensive
    • Short duration of action
    • Chance of allergy with repeated infusions

Pegloticase

  • 8mg every 2 weeks
  • effective at maintaining serum urate below target (up to 6 months)
  • Expensive
    • Short duration of action
    • Chance of allergy with repeated infusions

Corticosteroids & gout
Intra-articular steroid injection

  • Options
    • Betamethasone
    • Methylprednisolone
    • Triamcinolone
  • Injected after joint aspiration done through the same needle
    • Done with other therapy (e.g. colchicine 0.5mg twice, daily) as prophylaxis

Systemic corticosteroid delivery
Regime can be modified based on rapidity of response
Prednisone

  • 20mg/3 days -> 15mg/3 days -> 10mg/3 days -> 5mg/3 days -> 0mg
    • Need to wean
  • Consider:
    • diabetic patients (hyperglycaemia)
    • co-existing sepsis (risk of immunosuppression)
      • poor response

Tetracosactrin (ACTH)
Stimulates adrenal glands to produce & release endogenous corticosteroids (cortisol)

  • requires intact adrenal function (obviously)
  • endogenously reduce inflammation

Test adrenal function/reserve before giving depot preparation (intramuscular)

  • DO NOT USE if patient is on chronic prednisone therapy
  • Contraindicated in diabetic patients – ⇑ corticosteroid leads to hyperglycaemia
  • Useful if patient will not follow-up
    • Depot preparation - slow release, gradual absroption

Biologic agents
IL-1 inhibitors to suppress inflammation

  • Canakinumab
    • Effective but extremely expensive
Drug Advantages Disadvantages
Allopurinol - single daily dose Poor at eliminating tophi
- works in renal failure
Febuxostat - single daily dose Long-term safety & efficacy (it’s a new drug)
- reduced incidence of gouty ‘flares’
Benzbromarone - Effective in renal impairment - Liver toxicity
- Promotes uric acid stones
Probenecid - Patients with low uric acid secretion - Multiple daily dosing
- inffective in renal failure
- promotes uric acid stones
Uricase - Able to eliminate tophi - COST
- Effective in tumour lysis syndrome

Before adding urate-lowering therapy to gout patients, consider the following:

  • Culprit drugs
    • thiazide diuretics - increased risk of crystallisation
      • some diuretics reduce kidney excretion of urate
    • cyclosporin, tacrolimus (both immunosuppressants for transplant organ recipients)
      • decrease clearance of urate from kidney, cyclosporin > tacrolimus
    • pyrazinamide, ethambutol (anti-TB)
      • pyrazinamide is a strong urate retention agent
      • ethambutol decreases renal clearance of urate but less than the former
  • Weak uricosuric indication
    • fenofibrate - increases renal clearance of urate
    • losartan - increases urine pH, urate becomes more soluble

Pseudogout

Deposition of calcium pyrophosphate dihydrate (CPPD) crystals in the joint
Calcification of the soft tissues, tendons, bursae

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Most common chronic inflammatory joint disease
Females > Males
Peak onset - age 50 - 70

Smoking - strongest risk factor

Autoimmune
Gene polymorphisms (gene variants in population)
HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, HLA-DP, HLA-DM

Pannus formation at synovium

  • Increased pressure
    • hypoxia in type B synoviocytes (fibroblastic)
    • Hypoxia inducible factor (HIF1-a) increased
    • IL-1, TNF in type A synoviocytes (macrophages) stabilise HIF1-a
    • H1F1-a stimulates Vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF)
    • VEGF causes poorly ordered angiogenesis
    • Cytokine cascade from new endothelial vessels

Clinical Features

Early morning stiffness > 30 minutes
Synovitis in 3 or more joints
Symptoms last > 6 weeks

  • Viral infections can cause RA-like symptoms that usually resolve by 6 weeks

NEED TO EXCLUDE

  • Viral
  • Psoriatic
  • SLE
  • Crystal

Early onset OA presentation - looks non-specific

  • local soft tissue inflammation
    • carpal tunnel syndrome, bursitis
  • systemic features
    • fatigue
    • weight loss
    • fever
    • myalgia (muscle pain)

Joint distribution - UL

  • MCPs
  • PIPs - DIPs unaffected
  • Wrists
  • Elbows
  • Shoulders
  • Erosions at:
    • Joints of the thumb
    • 1st, 2nd MCP
    • 2nd PIP
    • Wrist joint

Joint distribution - LL

  • Hips
  • Knees
  • Ankle
  • MTPs

Typical RA deformities in the hand

Due to uncontrolled inflammation - joint & soft tissues damaged

  • ulnar deviation
  • subluxation at MCPs
  • Swan neck deformity - DIP extended, PIP flexed
  • Boutonniere - DIP flexed, PIP extended

Spinal involvement

  • Pannus at C1-C2
  • Atlanto-axial subluxation
  • Sub-axial subluxation
  • Potential compression of spinal cord from pannus growth off C1-C2

Extra-articular features of RA (outside joint)

Rheumatoid nodules - most common

  • presence of extra-articular features associated with worse prognosis
  • Sero-positive (RF, anti-CCP)
  • Typically occur on elbows
    • usually extensor surfaces
    • pressure areas – olecranon, lateral fingers, heel
    • not limited, can occur in Achilles tendon, lungs
  • Late RA, nodules have central necrosis/rim cells/outer pannus

Lung disease

  • Pleural effusions
  • Pleuritis
  • Fibrosis
  • Rheumatoid nodules

Heart disease

  • Pericarditis
  • Pericardial effusions

Eye disease

  • Scleromalacia perforans
    • Inflammation of sclera
    • Thinning & rupture of sclera
  • Sicca syndrome
    • dry eyes
    • dry mouth
    • Not everyone with Sicca has Sjogren’s

Vasculitis

  • Nail fold infarcts
  • Vasculitic ulcers
  • mononeuritis multiplex
    • loss of sensory & motor function of individual nerves

Felty’s syndrome
Occurs in long-standing, seropositive RA

  • Splenomegaly with/without lymphadenopathy
  • Neutropenia - abnormally few neutrophils
  • Leg ulcers
  • Recurrent infections

Morbidity & mortality in RA

Cardiovascular risk of RA

  • Increased risk of CVE from RA
    • Good control of inflammation reduces CVE risk
      • methotrexate, biologic agents
    • Smoking exacerbates CVE

Rest of spine rarely involved

Diagnostic tests

Autoantibodies

  • RF & anti-CCP
  • Useful for diagnosis & prognosis

Non-specific investigations

  • ESR & CRP
  • Markers of inflammation – useful for monitoring disease activity

Rheumatoid factor (RF)

  • Poor screening test
  • Low specificity in general population
    • false positive in patients > 75 years
  • Present in infections, other autoimmune diseases

Anti-CCP - antibody to cyclic citrullinated protein

  • Highly specific (more specific than RF)
  • Correlates with RF
    • Better than RF in predicting severity of disease
      • erosive disease
  • Early association with RA
  • HLA-DRB1 associated
    • HLA-DRB1*0402 is protective against RA
      • enables antigen presentation to CD4 Tcell
    • Influences development of seropositive RA
    • Associated with more severe RA, extra-articular manifestations

General investigations in RA
FBC

  • Normochromic (normal Hb concentration) normocytomic (normal sized RBCs) anaemia – just very little RBCs
  • Normal WCC
  • Thrombocytosis - too much platelets (sign of active RA)

ESR

  • Increased with disease activity
  • Increases with age - normal is around half a person’s age

CRP

  • acute phase reactant
  • increases with disease activity, not affected by age

Ferritin (protein that stores iron)

  • increases as acute phase reactant

Definite diagnosis of RA

  • Multiple joint involvement with synovitis
    • especially small joints of hands, feet
  • ESR and/or CRP elevated
  • Positive RF and/or CCP
    • Anti-CCP especially
  • Symptoms > 6 weeks
  • Need to exclude other diseases

Not all RA patients have anti-CCP, patients with parasitic/mycobacterial diseases may have anti-CCP as well

X-ray

L - loss of joint space - narrowing
E - erosions
S - soft tissue swelling
S - soft bones (osteopenia)

Early RA in Hands

  • soft tissue swelling
  • peri-articular osteoporosis
  • subtle erosions

Established RA

  • Punched out “bites” erosion
    • chronic, form sclerotic rim when healed

Late RA

  • Diffuse cartilage loss by pannus
  • Diffuse loss of joint space
  • Erosion of surface cartilage & margins by the pannus
  • Subchondral cysts - same as OA…
  • Synovial cyst

Hands

  • peri-articular erosion
  • Subluxation - base of thumb (but not OA)

Management of RA

Importance of early treatment

  • early damage
    • erosion in 2 years
    • significant BMD loss in 1st year
  • early disability

Aim of treatment

  • Remission or lowered disease activity
  • No stiffness, synovitis
  • low ESR/CRP
  • Symptomatic treatment
    • Analgesics
    • Anti-inflammatory drugs
    • NSAIDs (COX-2)
    • Corticosteroids
  • Begin with methotrexate
  • 2nd DMA after 3 months
  • Biologic agents after 6 months

Methotrexate

Purine antagonist - prevent RNA/DNA synthesis
Acts on dihydrofolate reductase (needed for de novo synthesis of purines)

  • up to 20mg weekly
  • folic acid supplements to reduce side effects:
    • mouth ulcers
    • nausea
    • abnormal liver function - accumulation in liver
    • Increased infection risk - bone marrow suppression
    • pneumonitis
    • fibrosis of lung
  • monitor FBC, LFT, creatinine

Leflunomide

Pyrimidine antagonist - prevent RNA/DNA synthesis
Acts on de novo synthesis pathway for pyrimidines (against dihydroorotate dehydrogenase)

  • Side effects
    • alopecia - hair loss/balding
    • diarrhoea
    • rash
    • abnormal liver function
    • infection risk
    • cytopenia - reduction of blood cell counts in general
    • peripheral neuropathy

Sulfasalazine

Used alone in sero-negative arthritis or in combination

  • Anti-inflammatory, 4 - 6 tablets per day
    • thought to inhibit NFkb (nuclear factor kappa B)
    • inhibits granulocyte activation & chemotaxis
    • inhibits synthesis of IL-1 & IL-2 (monocytes & lymphocytes, respectively)
    • inhibits lymphocyte proliferation
  • Side effects
    • Rash
    • nausea
    • headache
    • rare but serious - neutropenia
    • GI toxicity
    • dizziness
    • depression
    • thrombocytopenia
    • reversible infertility in males

Hydroxychloroquine

used in combination

  • Anti-malarial but has anti-inflammatory effects
    • targets lysosomes
      • prevents activation of innate immune cells
      • stops presentation to lymphocytes
  • Side effects - time limited use due to toxicity
    • GI toxicity
    • rash
    • rare retinal complication - chloroquine retinopathy
      • macular lesion
      • reduction in visual acuity

Biologic agents

  • TNF inhibitors (anti-TNF antibodies)
    • infliximab
    • etanercept
    • adalumimab
    • golimumab
    • certolizumab
      • TNF needed for granuloma formation
      • risk of reactivating latent TB

        Side effects of TNF inhibitors

        • Injection site reactions common
        • Infections - especially respiratory tract
        • Reactivation of latent infections e.g. TB
        • Demyelination
        • Drug-induced SLE
  • IL-6 antibodies
    • tocilizumab
      • blocking of IL-6 prevents JAK-STAT3 intracellular signalling - reducing cellular survival & proliferation

        Side effects

        • abnormal liver function
        • neutropenia
        • Increased infection risk - cellulitis, others
        • Increased cholesterol (HDL & LDL)

  • Anti-CD20 (B-cell blocker)

    • rituximab
      • induces ADCC

        Side effects

        • Pulmonary toxicity - rituximab-induced ILD

  • CTLA-4 antibody - prevents co-stimulation between T cell and APCs

    • abatacept
      • Blocks CD80/86 on APC, T-cells cannot activate & proliferate
      • Inhibits T-cell mediated cytokine release

        Side effects

        • immunosuppression - increased infections
        • hypersensitivity reactions
        • Respiratory function worsening in COPD patients
        • headache

Benefits of biologic agents
Good response from patients - increased remission rates
Reduced radiological damage
Normalisation of ESR, CRP
Reduced CVEs

DMARDs

Target immune cells & inflammatory pathways
Slow-acting
Long term outcome - slow progression of disease

Conventional DMARDs
Methotrexate & leflunomide (purine, pyrimidine synthesis inhibition respectively)
Sulfasalazine (anti-inflammatory, acts on NF-kb)
Hydroxychloroquine (anti-inflammatory)

Biological DMARDs
TNF-a inhibitors - infliximab
IL-6 inhibitors - tocilizumab
CD20 blocker (block B-cells) - rituximab
CTLA4 inhibitor (prevent co-stimulation between T-cell & APC) - abatacept

Psoriatic arthritis

Joints involved

  • Vertebrae
  • Sacroiliac joint
  • Hand
    • Asymmetrical
    • All joints of the hand
  • Ankle
  • Foot

Systemic lupus erythematosus

Autoimmune disease
Females of childbearing age > Males (9:1)

Management of SLE

Hydroxychloroquine (anti-inflammatory)
Short term prednisone (if not controlled with NSAIDs)
Azathiprine or methotrexate

Diffuse proliferative glomerulonephritis
High dose steroids + mycophenolate (immunosuppressant)

Specific auto-antibodies

  • Inflammatory markers
    • ESR
    • CRP
  • RF - in association with anti-CCP
  • Anti-CCP
  • Others (can be positive, usually negative)
    • ANA
    • ENA (SSA, SSB)
    • dsDNA

SLE

  • ANA + (non-specific)
  • ENA tests
    • Smith + (most specific)
    • SSA + (also present in Sjogren’s, SLE, RA)
    • SSB +
  • dsDNA + (specific, positive in 60% patients)

RF present, but non-specific

Scleroderma

Scl-70 - diffuse scleroderma
Anti-centromere - limited scleroderma

Sjogren’s

ENA - SSA, SSB
